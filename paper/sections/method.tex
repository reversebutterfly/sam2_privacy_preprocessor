\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Uniform Boundary Ring Suppression (BRS)}
\label{subsec:brs}

Let $\mathbf{F} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 3}$ be a video frame and $\mathbf{M} \in \{0,1\}^{H \times W}$ a binary object mask.
\emph{Boundary ring suppression} replaces the object boundary region with a blended background proxy:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:brs}
\mathbf{F}'_{ij} =
\begin{cases}
(1 - w_{ij}\alpha)\,\mathbf{F}_{ij} + w_{ij}\alpha\,\mathbf{B}_{ij} & \text{if } (i,j) \in \mathcal{R}(r) \\
\mathbf{F}_{ij} & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{R}(r)$ is a feathered ring of width $r$ pixels around the mask boundary, $\alpha \in [0,1]$ is the blend strength, $w_{ij} \in [0,1]$ is a smooth Gaussian taper, and $\mathbf{B}$ is a flat-mean background proxy (Gaussian-blurred average of the non-mask neighbourhood).
The flat-mean proxy creates a low-entropy boundary region that triggers H.264 DCT block mismatch artifacts upon codec compression, amplifying SAM2's boundary-feature degradation beyond the pre-codec edit alone.

\subsection{Anisotropic Sector Allocation}
\label{subsec:aniso}

Uniform BRS applies the same $\alpha$ around the entire boundary.
We hypothesise that SAM2's boundary sensitivity is \emph{directionally non-uniform}: some boundary directions are more critical for tracking than others, and concentrating the suppression budget there should yield larger degradation at the same visual cost.

\paragraph{Angular sector parametrisation.}
We divide the boundary annulus into $K{=}8$ angular sectors based on the angle from the mask centroid.
Each sector $k$ receives an independent blend strength $\alpha_k \in [0,1]$, while the radial profile (ring width $r$, Gaussian taper) remains shared.
The per-pixel blend weight becomes:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sector}
w^\mathrm{sector}_{ij} = w^\mathrm{ring}_{ij} \cdot \sum_{k=1}^{K} \alpha_k \cdot s_k(i,j),
\end{equation}
where $s_k(i,j)$ is a smoothed indicator for sector $k$ (Gaussian-blurred hard sector assignment, $\sigma{=}3$\,px to avoid hard boundaries).

\paragraph{Strict iso-budget constraint.}
To isolate the effect of \emph{directional} allocation from \emph{total} budget, we enforce exact area-weighted budget matching:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:budget}
\frac{\sum_{k=1}^{K} A_k \cdot \alpha_k}{\sum_{k=1}^{K} A_k} = \alpha_\mathrm{target},
\end{equation}
where $A_k = \sum_{(i,j)} w^\mathrm{ring}_{ij} \cdot s_k(i,j)$ is the effective ring area in sector $k$, and $\alpha_\mathrm{target}{=}0.80$ matches the uniform BRS baseline.
We enforce this via iterative water-filling projection (shift-and-clip), which converges to exact budget within $10^{-5}$ in ${\leq}50$ iterations.
This guarantee means any improvement of the anisotropic allocation over uniform BRS is \emph{purely} due to directional redistribution, not increased total distortion.

\paragraph{Oracle search.}
For each video, we search for the best $\boldsymbol{\alpha}{=}(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_K)$ using random sampling (10 candidates including structured patterns) followed by budget-projected coordinate-pair refinement (6 steps).
Each candidate is evaluated end-to-end: BRS edit $\to$ H.264 CRF=23 encode/decode $\to$ SAM2 tracking $\to$ $\Delta\mathrm{JF}_\mathrm{codec}$.
All candidates satisfy the exact budget constraint (Eq.~\ref{eq:budget}).

\subsection{Two-Stage Learned Predictor}
\label{subsec:predictor}

The oracle search requires per-video SAM2 inference and is not scalable.
We train a lightweight predictor to approximate the oracle allocation from first-frame features alone.

\paragraph{Features (60-dim).}
From the first annotated frame and mask, we extract:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Global (4):} mask area ratio, aspect ratio, compactness ($4\pi A / P^2$), boundary length ratio.
  \item \textbf{Per-sector (7$\times$8):} ring area fraction, mean RGB (3 channels), mean gradient magnitude, boundary curvature proxy (gradient direction variance), and optical flow magnitude (Farneb\"ack flow to second frame).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Architecture.}
A two-stage MLP:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Gate} ($60 \to 64 \to 1$): predicts whether anisotropic allocation benefits this video. Below a confidence threshold, the predictor falls back to uniform $\alpha{=}\alpha_\mathrm{target}$, reducing downside risk by defaulting to the uniform BRS baseline.
  \item \textbf{Sector head} ($60 \to 64 \to 64 \to 8$): predicts sector logits $\to$ sigmoid $\to$ water-filling projection to satisfy Eq.~\ref{eq:budget}.
\end{enumerate}
Training minimises MSE between projected predictions and oracle alphas, plus a binary cross-entropy gate loss (positive label if oracle gap $> 5\,\mathrm{pp}$).
The gate provides a risk-reduction mechanism: on uncertain videos, it defaults to uniform BRS rather than risking a harmful allocation.

\subsection{Codec Pipeline and Evaluation Protocol}
\label{subsec:protocol}

All privacy and tracking metrics are evaluated \emph{post-codec}:
preprocessed frames $\to$ FFmpeg libx264 CRF=23 encode/decode $\to$ SAM2 tracking with first-frame point prompt.
The primary metric $\Delta\mathrm{JF}_\mathrm{codec} = \mathrm{JF}_\mathrm{codec\text{-}clean} - \mathrm{JF}_\mathrm{codec\text{-}adv}$ isolates attack effectiveness from codec-induced tracking degradation on clean video.
Quality is measured by SSIM (capped at 20 frames) and LPIPS~\citep{zhang2018lpips}.
