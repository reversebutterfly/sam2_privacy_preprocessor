\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Video datasets underpin advances in computer vision, but their publication enables automated tracking of subjects without consent.
SAM2~\citep{ravi2024sam2} makes this especially easy: given a single-frame prompt, it propagates segmentation masks across entire videos with high accuracy.
A natural defense is \emph{publisher-side privacy preprocessing}: a lightweight edit applied before release that degrades SAM2 tracking while preserving the video's utility for other research tasks.

For such preprocessing to be deployable, it must survive the standard distribution channel.
Video datasets are almost universally compressed with H.264 (CRF~23) before release---YouTube, institutional repositories, and data-sharing platforms all apply this step.
High-frequency adversarial perturbations, which form the basis of prior attacks such as UAP-SAM2~\citep{uap_sam2_2025}, are quantized to zero by H.264's DCT-based compression, rendering them ineffective post-codec.

\emph{Boundary ring suppression} (BRS) addresses this by replacing high-frequency boundary features with low-frequency, codec-surviving content: a locally inpainted background blended into a narrow ring around the object boundary.
Uniform BRS---applying the same blend strength $\alpha$ around the entire boundary---is already effective: at $(r{=}24\,\mathrm{px}, \alpha{=}0.93)$, it degrades SAM2 tracking by $+53.0\,\mathrm{pp}$ on DAVIS ($n{=}74$, post-codec).
But this uniform allocation is suboptimal.

\paragraph{Central finding: anisotropy matters more than magnitude.}
We show that at \emph{fixed distortion budget}---the same area-weighted total blend strength---redirecting suppression toward SAM2-vulnerable boundary directions yields dramatically larger tracking degradation.
On DAVIS ($n{=}40$), angular-sector oracle allocation achieves $+56.9\,\mathrm{pp}$ versus $+20.1\,\mathrm{pp}$ for uniform BRS at exact iso-budget ($=0.80$), a gap of $+36.8\,\mathrm{pp}$ with $100\%$ win-rate and near-identical SSIM.
This is not search noise: SAM2's boundary-sensitive encoder~\citep{ravi2024sam2} has directionally non-uniform vulnerability, and the oracle consistently exploits this structure.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Anisotropic boundary allocation as the key factor.}
  We demonstrate, via strict iso-budget oracle experiments ($n{=}40$ DAVIS, exact area-weighted water-filling projection, $100\%$ win-rate, $+36.8\,\mathrm{pp}$ gap), that directional budget allocation dominates uniform BRS at the same quality cost.

  \item \textbf{Matched-quality baseline comparison.}
  We compare five boundary-localised edit operators (BRS, pixelation, inpainting, morphological fill, boundary blur) at matched SSIM \emph{and} LPIPS, revealing that pixelation is the strongest uniform operator---and that directional allocation provides gains on top of it.

  \item \textbf{Complete empirical package.}
  Full DAVIS sweep ($n{=}74$), YT-VOS ($n{=}497$, $91.9\%$ wins), HEVC ($+28.5\,\mathrm{pp}$), XMem ($17.6\,\mathrm{pp}$), and an honest privacy-quality frontier showing the $\mathrm{SSIM}{\geq}0.95$ ceiling at ${\sim}20\,\mathrm{pp}$.

  \item \textbf{Honest scope and limitations.}
  We frame this as publisher-side, mask-conditioned, SAM2-targeted preprocessing---not a general-purpose adversarial attack---and report utility costs explicitly (YOLO recall $-22\,\mathrm{pp}$).
\end{enumerate}
