\section{Limitations and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
This work is scoped to publisher-side, mask-conditioned preprocessing targeting SAM2---not a general-purpose adversarial attack.
The anisotropic allocation is validated on DAVIS ($n{=}40$) with H.264 CRF=23 and point prompt; cross-codec and cross-prompt validation of the anisotropy (not just uniform BRS) is future work.
The learned predictor achieves $31\%$ oracle gap closure with $n{=}22$ training videos and hand-crafted features; scaling to more training data and richer features (temporal, deep) should improve this.
The flat-mean background proxy is central to the mechanism; natural-texture proxies (which improve visual quality) do not trigger the same codec artifacts and are substantially less effective.
Downstream utility cost ($-22\,\mathrm{pp}$ YOLO recall) is real and deliberate.

\paragraph{Conclusion.}
We showed that for codec-robust publisher-side privacy preprocessing, \textbf{directional allocation of boundary suppression matters more than total suppression strength}.
At fixed distortion budget, anisotropic allocation yields $+36.8\,\mathrm{pp}$ more tracking degradation than uniform BRS ($100\%$ win-rate, $n{=}40$ DAVIS, strict iso-budget).
A lightweight gated predictor can learn to exploit this gap from first-frame features alone ($90\%$ win-rate, $+17.6\,\mathrm{pp}$ mean gain on held-out videos).
These findings reframe privacy preprocessing as a \emph{budget allocation} problem rather than a magnitude problem, and suggest that understanding SAM2's directionally non-uniform boundary vulnerability is the key to stronger privacy at lower visual cost.
