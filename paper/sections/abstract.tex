We study publisher-side privacy preprocessing for video datasets: a lightweight step applied before H.264/HEVC compression that must reliably degrade SAM2-based video object tracking while bounding visual quality loss.

Our central finding is that \textbf{directional budget allocation matters more than the choice of edit operator}: at the \emph{same} area-weighted distortion budget, redirecting any boundary-localised edit toward SAM2-vulnerable angular directions yields dramatically larger tracking degradation than applying it uniformly.
On DAVIS ($n{=}16$, strict per-frame iso-budget with exact water-filling projection to budget${=}0.80$), sector-wise oracle allocation of flat-mean boundary suppression achieves $+51.2\,\mathrm{pp}$ mean $\Delta\mathrm{JF}_\mathrm{codec}$ versus $+20.1\,\mathrm{pp}$ for the uniform baseline---a $+31.1\,\mathrm{pp}$ gap at $100\%$ win-rate with near-identical SSIM ($\Delta\mathrm{SSIM} \leq 0.01$).

We provide a matched-quality baseline comparison across five edit operators (boundary ring suppression, pixelation, inpainting, morphological fill, boundary blur), evaluated with both SSIM and LPIPS to ensure perceptual fairness.
Full DAVIS paired results ($n{=}74$) confirm the strength of codec-amplified boundary editing: the best uniform operators achieve $34$--$66\,\mathrm{pp}$ post-codec tracking degradation at $\mathrm{SSIM} \approx 0.93$.
The privacy-quality tradeoff is reported honestly: under a strict high-fidelity constraint ($\mathrm{SSIM}{\geq}0.95$), the attack ceiling is approximately $20\,\mathrm{pp}$ for any uniform method, confirming that the anisotropic direction is the path to stronger privacy at high fidelity.
