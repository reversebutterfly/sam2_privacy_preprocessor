We investigate whether pixel-constrained adversarial perturbations ($\varepsilon = 8/255$, $L_\infty$) can survive H.264 video codec compression to disrupt SAM2-based video object tracking---a scenario relevant to publisher-side privacy protection for video datasets. Through seven systematic experiments across two attack paradigms (four rounds of pixel-level learned preprocessors and three modes of feature-space direct optimization) and four candidate attack insertion points (pixel space, FPN features, memory encoder tokens, and object pointers), we find a consistent negative result within the tested budget and attack families: \textbf{H.264 CRF23 neutralizes all tested attack strategies}. The mean tracking degradation under codec ($\djfauc$) falls within $[-0.004, +0.004]$ across all experiments, well below the viable threshold of $0.03$. Notably, attacks succeed in isolation---a single-frame perturbation collapses SAM2 tracking from $93.5\%$ to $6.8\%$ JF score without codec ($\djfadv = +0.867$)---but this effect vanishes completely after H.264 encoding ($\djfauc = -0.002$). We characterize H.264 CRF23 as an effective \emph{adversarial purifier} in this threat model, identify the high-frequency concentration of $L_\infty$-constrained perturbations as the root cause of codec fragility, and discuss implications for adversarial privacy methods and video robustness evaluation.
