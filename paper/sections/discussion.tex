\section{Discussion, Limitations, and Implications}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Implications for Privacy-Preserving Video}

Our findings place a fundamental constraint on the adversarial approach to publisher-side privacy protection for SAM2-based tracking. Within the $\varepsilon = 8/255$ L$_\infty$ budget, the standard video release pipeline (H.264 CRF23) acts as an effective adversarial purifier that the attacker cannot overcome with current gradient-based methods. Future work in this direction should consider:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Larger $\varepsilon$}: At sufficient perturbation magnitude, low-frequency pixel energy would survive codec. However, the SSIM and LPIPS constraints would also be violated, making the perturbation visible. The exact crossover point is an open question.
  \item \textbf{Semantic-level perturbations}: Perturbations that directly modify low-frequency video statistics (e.g., color distribution, spatial composition) may survive codec at lower $\varepsilon$, at the cost of changing video appearance more dramatically.
  \item \textbf{Alternative codecs}: VP9 and H.265 have different quantization profiles. CRF23 H.264 is the most relevant for practical video distribution, but robustness may differ across codec settings.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implications for Adversarial Robustness Evaluation}

Our results suggest that adversarial attacks against video segmentation and tracking should be evaluated under real codec conditions. UAP-SAM2~\citep{uap_sam2_2025}, DarkSAM~\citep{darksam2024}, and related work report results under standard (codec-free) conditions. If these systems are intended for deployment in real video pipelines, their attack effectiveness may be substantially reduced by the codec that is an unavoidable part of video distribution.

We recommend that video adversarial attack papers include a ``post-codec'' column in their main results tables, using real FFmpeg H.264 CRF23 (or the target deployment codec) rather than a differentiable proxy. Our differentiable proxy (YUV 4:2:0 + blur + noise + resize) is a reasonable training augmentation but is not a faithful evaluation substitute for real H.264.

\subsection{Limitations}

\paragraph{Single backbone.} All experiments use SAM2.1 hiera\_tiny. Larger models (SAM2-Small, SAM2-Large) have different feature-space characteristics and may exhibit different codec-robustness behavior.

\paragraph{Small validation set.} Nine DAVIS videos provide clear trends but limited statistical power. Multi-dataset validation (YT-VOS, MOSE) would strengthen the negative result.

\paragraph{Surrogate gap in pixel-level attacks.} The pixel-level training surrogate uses \texttt{logits.detach()} and a single rolling memory entry, approximating but not exactly replicating the full SAM2VideoPredictor computation graph. We cannot rule out that exact end-to-end gradient flow would produce different results, though the feature-space direct optimization experiments (which are closer to exact local optimization) also fail.

\paragraph{Dataset selection bias.} The validity filter ($\text{JF}_\text{clean} \geq 0.5$) retains 9 DAVIS videos where SAM2 tracks well under clean conditions. Harder videos (lower $\text{JF}_\text{clean}$) might exhibit different codec-robustness dynamics; however, they also represent less relevant attack targets (if SAM2 already fails cleanly, perturbing them provides no privacy benefit).

\paragraph{Single CRF setting.} Results are for CRF23. Very high quality (CRF18) would attenuate less; lower quality (CRF28) would attenuate more. A sweep across CRF values would strengthen the characterization of codec attenuation as a function of compression strength. CRF23 is the most practically relevant setting for public video distribution.

\paragraph{Adaptive codec-aware optimization (most important limitation).}
Our differentiable H.264 proxy is used as a \emph{training regularizer}, not as a fully differentiable codec. A truly adaptive attacker with access to BPDA-style gradients~\citep{athalye2018obfuscated} through the real codec, or EOT-style robustness via compression augmentation, might produce perturbations with more low-frequency structure. We note that our feature-space direct optimization already uses the proxy during optimization, yet still fails; however, we cannot rule out that a more faithful gradient estimator through real H.264 would yield different results. This is the most important open question following from our work.

\paragraph{Scope.} This is not an impossibility proof. We have shown that specific attack families fail within $\varepsilon = 8/255$. A fundamentally different attack paradigm—including stronger adaptive optimization, larger budgets, or semantic-level manipulation—might succeed.

\subsection{Connection to Adversarial Purification}

The codec-kill phenomenon we document is conceptually related to adversarial purification~\citep{yoon2021adv_purification,diffpure2022}, where a preprocessing step (typically a generative model) removes adversarial perturbations before the target classifier. Our finding shows this purification can occur ``for free'' as a side effect of lossy video compression. This has implications for the broader question of natural robustness: if a compression channel exists in the deployment pipeline, adversarial perturbations optimized for the pre-compression distribution may fail catastrophically in the post-compression regime.
