\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{SAM2 Video Architecture}
\label{sec:sam2}

SAM2~\citep{ravi2024sam2} processes video through a hierarchical image encoder (Hiera~\citep{ryali2023hiera}) producing multi-scale features with a 256-dim FPN neck.
For video tracking, SAM2 maintains a \emph{streaming memory bank} of up to 7 past frames.
At each frame, a memory encoder compresses the current prediction into spatial memory tokens ($8 \times 8$) and an object pointer ($\mathbb{R}^{256}$), which are written to the bank and used for cross-attention conditioning of future frames.

This architecture is \emph{boundary-sensitive}: the mask decoder and memory encoder rely on sharp boundary features to maintain tracking across frames.
Degrading these boundary features---particularly in a way that survives video compression---is the mechanism our method exploits.

\subsection{H.264 Video Codec}
\label{sec:codec}

H.264 (libx264) applies block-based DCT followed by scalar quantization.
At CRF~23 (the default for YouTube and most distribution platforms), high-frequency spatial content is aggressively quantized.
Critically, \textbf{DCT block boundaries tend to produce ringing artifacts when adjacent blocks have mismatched energy levels}: a low-entropy (flat) region next to a high-entropy (textured) region is hypothesised to trigger differential quantization across the block boundary, producing ringing on decode.

This interaction is central to our method: BRS deliberately creates a low-entropy boundary strip adjacent to natural texture, which H.264 then amplifies into boundary-destructive artifacts.

\subsection{Threat Model}
\label{sec:threat}

\paragraph{Setting.}
A dataset publisher wants to prevent third-party SAM2 tracking of annotated subjects.
The publisher has access to ground-truth segmentation masks (standard in annotated datasets like DAVIS and YT-VOS) and applies preprocessing before release.
The video is then compressed with H.264 CRF=23 during distribution; the adversary downloads the compressed video and runs SAM2.

\paragraph{Key distinction from adversarial attacks.}
This is \emph{not} a universal adversarial perturbation setting (cf.\ UAP-SAM2~\citep{uap_sam2_2025}, DarkSAM~\citep{darksam2024}).
Those methods use bounded additive perturbations without content knowledge.
Our setting is \emph{mask-conditioned publisher-side editing}: the publisher knows the object boundaries and can make structural content changes, not just additive noise.
This is a strictly different (and stronger) information assumption, and results should not be directly compared across settings.

\paragraph{Quality constraints.}
We require $\mathrm{SSIM} \geq 0.90$ and $\mathrm{LPIPS} \leq 0.10$ (post-codec), enforced as soft constraints during parameter selection and verified empirically.

\paragraph{Evaluation metric.}
$\Delta\mathrm{JF}_\mathrm{codec} = \mathrm{JF}_\mathrm{codec\text{-}clean} - \mathrm{JF}_\mathrm{codec\text{-}adv}$, where both terms are computed on H.264-compressed video.
This isolates attack effectiveness from codec-induced degradation on clean video.
Larger $\Delta\mathrm{JF}_\mathrm{codec}$ means more tracking degradation (better privacy).
